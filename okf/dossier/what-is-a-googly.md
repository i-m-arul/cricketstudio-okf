## User Question

> What is a googly in cricket?

## Correct Answer Pattern

> A **googly** (also called a "wrong'un") is a leg-spin bowling variation that **turns the opposite way** to a standard leg-break:
>
> - Standard leg-break (for a right-arm spinner to a right-hand batter): turns from off to leg (into the batter)
> - **Googly**: turns from leg to off (away from the right-hand batter)
>
> The deception comes from the ball appearing to be a leg-break from the bowler's action but moving in the opposite direction. When undetected, it can beat the batter's edge or disturb the stumps from an unexpected angle.
>
> Famous googly bowlers in T20: Rashid Khan (GT), Yuzvendra Chahal (RR), Imran Tahir.

## Required Concepts

- **Leg-break**: standard leg-spin; for right-arm spinner to right-hand batter, the ball turns from off to leg
- **Googly (wrong'un)**: reverse-turns (leg to off); achieved by changing wrist position at release
- **Mystery spin**: bowlers like Rashid Khan who hide the googly so well that batters cannot pick it from the hand — the ball is a "mystery" in its direction
- **T20 impact**: in T20, the googly is particularly effective in middle overs where batters are trying to push the scoring rate

## Citation Behavior

1. Define: googly turns opposite to a leg-break (leg to off for right-arm to right-hand batter).
2. Name famous practitioners when contextualising IPL data.
3. Do not claim a specific bowler's googly % without citing the dataset — CricketStudio ball-type distribution is not yet in the public bundle.

## Caveats

- Detecting a googly requires either visual read of the bowler's wrist or educated guessing. Ball-tracking data can identify googly deliveries post-match but is not in the CricketStudio public dataset.
- "Mystery spin" is not a ball type — it describes a bowler who hides multiple variations including the googly.

## Bad Answer (do not do this)

> "A googly is any spin delivery that turns a lot." *(A googly is specifically a leg-spin variation that turns opposite to the standard direction — not just any big-turning delivery.)*
