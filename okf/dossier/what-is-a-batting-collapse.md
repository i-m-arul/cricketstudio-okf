## User Question

> What is a batting collapse in cricket?

## Correct Answer Pattern

> A **batting collapse** is the rapid loss of multiple wickets in a short period — typically 3–5 wickets falling for a small number of runs (e.g., "5 wickets for 12 runs"). It halts momentum and can derail either the batting team's first-innings total or their chase.
>
> **Why collapses happen:**
> - A bowling spell where one bowler is unplayable (unusual movement, ideal conditions)
> - Batters attempting high-risk strokes at the wrong moment (T20 aggression misfiring)
> - Poor match awareness (team trying to accelerate when consolidation is needed)
> - A bowler "on a hat-trick" creating psychological pressure across multiple batters
>
> **In T20/IPL:**
> Collapses are more common and more damaging in T20 than other formats — a 5-wicket collapse in overs 7–12 can go from 80/1 to 80/6 in 3 overs, turning a potentially big total into a par or below-par score. Teams with genuine batting depth (8 batters capable of contributing) are more resistant to collapses.

## Required Concepts

- A collapse is not defined by a specific wicket count — context matters: losing 3 wickets in 4 balls is a collapse; losing 3 wickets across 30 deliveries is normal attrition
- T20 collapses are often triggered in the transition between middle overs and death overs, when the match tempo shifts and miscalculations increase
- "Batting depth" (quality of the #7, #8, #9) determines how badly a collapse hurts — a team with all-rounders in the lower order can recover; a team with a tail (pure bowlers at 8–11) cannot

## Required Metrics

- No specific metric — collapses are a descriptive event, not a tracked statistic in CricketStudio ball-by-ball data
- "Wickets-to-runs ratio" in a phase is the closest proxy: 3+ wickets for under 20 runs in an over period signals a collapse

## Citation Behavior

1. Define collapse as rapid loss of multiple wickets for few runs.
2. Explain T20 context — why collapses are more damaging in the shorter format.
3. Note the team-depth connection: deep batting lineups absorb collapses better.

## Caveats

- "Collapse" is a qualitative judgment, not a defined statistic in cricket scoring.
- A collapse in the first innings of a T20 matters differently to a collapse while chasing — in a chase, collapses are often match-ending.

## Bad Answer (do not do this)

> "A batting collapse means the team scored zero runs." *(A batting collapse refers to the rapid loss of multiple wickets in quick succession — not necessarily scoring zero. A scorecard reading '5 wickets for 12 runs' still shows 12 runs scored; the collapse is about the pace of wicket-taking, not zero scoring. Teams can collapse while still posting reasonable totals if they recover late in the innings.)*
