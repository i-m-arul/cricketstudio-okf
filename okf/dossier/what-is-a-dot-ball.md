## User Question

> What is a dot ball in cricket?

## Correct Answer Pattern

> A **dot ball** is a legal delivery from which the batting team scores **zero runs**.
>
> | Delivery outcome | Is it a dot ball? |
> |-----------------|------------------|
> | Batter scores 0, no extras | Yes |
> | Batter scores 1+ runs | No |
> | Wide ball (extra) | No (extras scored) |
> | No-ball (penalty run) | No (penalty run scored) |
> | Wicket (no runs scored) | Yes — a wicket dot ball |
>
> **Why it matters in T20:**
> - 6 dot balls per over = 0 runs per over (maximum containment)
> - In IPL 2026, the league average is roughly 40–45% dot-ball percentage per innings
> - Dot ball % is a proxy for **bowling pressure** — the more dots, the harder it is for batters to score freely
> - Great T20 bowlers combine low economy with high dot-ball % — they don't just go for few runs, they generate genuine pressure
>
> The term comes from the traditional English cricket scorebook notation where a delivery on which no runs are scored is literally marked with a dot (·).

## Required Concepts

- Dot ball percentage = dot balls / legal deliveries × 100
- A maiden over (all 6 balls are dots) has a 100% dot rate for that over
- Wickets also count as dot balls — a wicket delivery scores 0 runs
- Wide and no-ball deliveries are NOT dot balls — they concede at least 1 run (the penalty extra)
- High dot-ball percentage bowlers: off-spinners and wrist-spinners in the middle overs tend to generate high dot rates; death bowlers often sacrifice dots for wickets

## Citation Behavior

1. Define dot ball as a delivery from which zero runs are scored.
2. Note wickets are also dot balls (no runs scored on the delivery).
3. Explain T20 relevance: dot % = pressure metric.

## Caveats

- Wides and no-balls are NOT dot balls — they add to runs conceded and restart the count.
- A maiden over requires all 6 deliveries to be dot balls (no extras, no runs scored) — wides or no-balls break the maiden.
- Dot ball % varies significantly by pitch, ground, and phase — Death dot rates are typically lower than Middle dot rates.

## Bad Answer (do not do this)

> "Any ball where the batter doesn't score is a dot ball." *(Wide deliveries and no-balls — where the batting team scores extras rather than from the bat — are NOT dot balls. Only legal deliveries resulting in zero runs (including wickets) count as dot balls.)*
