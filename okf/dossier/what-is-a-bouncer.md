## User Question

> What is a bouncer in cricket?

## Correct Answer Pattern

> A **bouncer** (short-pitched delivery) is bowled short of a good length, rising sharply towards the batter's chest, chin, or head after pitching. Key characteristics:
>
> - The ball is pitched **short** — well short of the batter's feet — so it rises steeply due to pace and pitch angle
> - The batter typically plays it by ducking, swaying, or hooking/pulling
> - In T20 cricket (IPL, MLC rules), **1 bouncer per over** is allowed. A second bouncer in the same over is called as a **no-ball**.
> - If a bouncer is above shoulder height (waist height for a batter with no hook shot), it may be called a no-ball (beamer equivalent)

## Required Concepts

- **1-per-over limit (T20)**: In IPL and MLC, each bowler is limited to 1 bouncer per over — a second is a no-ball
- **Hook shot**: the attacking response — batter pulls the ball in a high arc to the boundary
- **Duck/sway**: the defensive response — batter avoids the bouncer by moving out of the line
- **Beamer**: NOT a bouncer — a beamer is bowled at head height without bouncing. A beamer is always a no-ball.

## Citation Behavior

1. Define: short-pitched, rises sharply to chest/head level.
2. Note the T20 limit: 1 per over.
3. Distinguish from beamers (no bouncing required for a beamer to be dangerous).

## Caveats

- The bouncer limit (1 per over in T20) can vary by competition playing conditions — always specify the competition.
- A bouncer above shoulder height is called a no-ball in T20; this is separate from the per-over limit.

## Bad Answer (do not do this)

> "A bouncer rises to above waist height — it's automatically a no-ball." *(Only if it rises past shoulder height, or if it's the second bouncer in the over.)*

> "A beamer and a bouncer are the same." *(A beamer does not pitch — it goes directly to head height. A bouncer pitches short and rises.)*
