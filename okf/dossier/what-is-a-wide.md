## User Question

> What is a wide in cricket?

## Correct Answer Pattern

> A **wide** is a delivery bowled outside the reach of the batter in their normal batting stance. When called:
>
> 1. **1 run** is added to the batting team's extras (charged to the bowler).
> 2. The delivery **must be re-bowled** — it does not count as one of the 6 balls in the over.
> 3. The batter **cannot hit** a wide (if they move and play it, it may be re-called by the umpire).
> 4. **No free hit** — unlike a no-ball, a wide does not trigger a free hit in T20.
>
> In T20 cricket, the wide line is applied more strictly than in Test/ODI cricket — the batter's guard position matters, and umpires use the crease lines as reference.

## Required Concepts

- **Wide line**: in T20, umpires call a delivery wide if it passes outside the return crease (the side line) when the batter is in a normal stance
- **Stumping off a wide**: a batter CAN be stumped off a wide — unlike a no-ball, dismissal by stumping is possible off a wide
- **Wide vs no-ball economy**: wide runs ARE charged to the bowler's economy. No-ball runs are also charged to the bowler.
- **No free hit**: a wide does not trigger a free hit (only a no-ball does in T20)

## Citation Behavior

1. State: 1 run added, delivery re-bowled, no free hit.
2. Note: stumping IS possible off a wide (unlike a no-ball where the batter can only be run-out).

## Caveats

- In Test cricket, the wide line is more lenient — deliveries that would be wides in T20 may be legal in Tests.
- T20 wide rules (strict line) incentivise bowlers to bowl to targets rather than outside the line — changes the bowling strategy.

## Bad Answer (do not do this)

> "A batter can be bowled off a wide." *(You cannot be bowled off a wide — the ball doesn't count; but stumping IS possible.)*

> "A wide triggers a free hit." *(Only no-balls trigger free hits in T20 — not wides.)*
