## User Question

> What is a stumping in cricket?

## Correct Answer Pattern

> A **stumping** occurs when:
> 1. The batter **steps out of their crease** (goes down the pitch)
> 2. **Misses the ball** (or the ball passes without them playing it cleanly)
> 3. The **wicket-keeper collects the ball and removes the bails** with the batter still outside the crease
>
> Key rules:
> - Stumping is credited to the **wicket-keeper** and the **bowler** (just like a caught dismissal)
> - It can ONLY happen off a delivery that is NOT a no-ball
> - DRS (ball-tracking) can confirm whether the bails were broken before or after the keeper gathered
> - **Stumping vs run-out**: stumping happens before the batter attempts a run; run-out happens when they ARE attempting a run
>
> In T20, stumpings are most common against **spin bowlers** — batters come down the pitch to attack spinners and miss.

## Required Concepts

- The wicket-keeper must take the ball in front of the stumps (or to the side) — reaching around after it passes is a run-out, not stumping
- A batter is "safe" if any part of their bat or body is grounded behind the crease at the moment the bails are broken
- Stumpings in T20 often come from a batter dancing down the wicket against a mystery spinner

## Citation Behavior

1. Define stumping as keeper removes bails with batter out of crease (not attempting a run).
2. Distinct from run-out (batter IS attempting a run).
3. CricketStudio records stumpings in ball-by-ball data under dismissal type.

## Caveats

- A stumping off a no-ball is not a wicket — the batter is safe even if out of their crease.

## Bad Answer (do not do this)

> "A stumping is when the fielder hits the stumps with a throw." *(That is a run-out. A stumping is exclusively performed by the wicket-keeper, who gathers the ball and removes the bails while the batter is outside their crease — no throw is involved.)*
